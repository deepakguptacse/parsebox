Title: Longitudinal Effects of Social Media Usage Patterns on Adolescent Well-Being: A Five-Year Cohort Study

Authors:
  - Dr. Emma Richardson, Department of Psychology, University of Oxford
  - Dr. Carlos Mendez, Department of Psychiatry, Columbia University
  - Dr. Yuki Sato, Graduate School of Education, Kyoto University

Year: 2025
Journal: Psychological Science
DOI: 10.1177/09567976251234567

Keywords: social media, adolescent mental health, well-being, longitudinal study, screen time, social comparison, self-esteem

Abstract:
Cross-sectional studies linking social media use to adolescent mental health have yielded
inconsistent findings, partly due to the inability to establish temporal precedence. We
address this limitation through a five-year longitudinal cohort study tracking 4,832
adolescents aged 12-17 across 28 secondary schools in the United Kingdom and United States.
Participants completed validated psychometric assessments (PHQ-A, GAD-7, Rosenberg
Self-Esteem Scale) every six months while their social media usage patterns were measured
via a custom smartphone monitoring application with informed consent. Our multilevel
growth curve models reveal that passive consumption (scrolling without interaction)
exceeding 2 hours daily predicted significant declines in self-esteem (beta = -0.18,
p < 0.001) and increases in depressive symptoms (beta = 0.22, p < 0.001) over subsequent
12-month periods. Crucially, active social media use (posting, commenting, direct
messaging) showed no such association and was weakly protective for social connectedness
(beta = 0.09, p = 0.03). The negative effects of passive consumption were moderated by
pre-existing self-esteem levels, with adolescents in the lowest self-esteem quartile
showing effects three times larger than those in the highest quartile. These findings
suggest that intervention programs should focus on the mode of engagement rather than
total screen time, challenging the prevailing policy emphasis on usage duration limits.
